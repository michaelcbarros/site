Institute

Waypoint Institute

Waypoint Institute is a collaborative research studio and publishing lab dedicated to mapping sacred imagination across culture. Borrowing the compass-driven palette of waypoint.institute, the project convenes scholars, artists, and community leaders to navigate questions of religion, civic storytelling, and public imagination.

Programming blends salons, residencies, and field reports that anchor spiritual inquiry in contemporary media and civic life.

Initiatives

  • Compass salons on religion, imagination, and civic futures.
  • Limited-run publications and audio briefings on emergent sacred cartographies.
  • Advisory partnerships with cultural institutions and research collaborators.

Focus areas

  • Religion & civic storytelling
  • Grounded cognition & imagination
  • Media ecologies & spiritual formation

In Progress

The Legend of Zelda & Religion

This book project examines The Legend of Zelda as a site of ritual, sacrifice, and mythopoesis. It traces how Nintendo’s series stages sacred time, liturgical repetition, and heroic vocation through interactive storytelling.

Research draws on liturgical studies, ritual theory, and game design analysis to link Hyrule’s geography with embodied religious imagination.

Research threads

  • Temporal loops as liturgical calendars in franchise timelines.
  • Sacred geography, pilgrimage, and shrine networks across Hyrule.
  • Embodied play as ritual rehearsal of vocation and sacrifice.

Outputs

  • Book proposal and chapter drafts (2025).
  • Conference papers on Zelda’s ritual architectures.
  • Public essays connecting game worlds to theological discourse.
Dissertation

Formation of Supernatural Agents in Dreams Through Simulation

Status
Doctoral dissertation, National University (ABD, proposal stage, 2025)
Chair
Dr. Patrick McNamara

This study advances a grounded cognition account of how concepts of supernatural agents are constructed in dreams and later reengaged in waking life. It contrasts modular “agency detection” explanations with an embodied, simulation-based mechanism that foregrounds affect and situated valuation.

Using secondary analysis of a longitudinal dataset (n=120 adults; ~10–14 days; ~1,200 dream reports; n=60 with DREEM REM data), dream texts are coded for sensorimotor simulation richness, agentic figures, and narrative structure. Multiple regression models test whether simulation richness predicts scores on religiosity (BMMRS) and paranormal belief (RPBS), and whether REM sleep dynamics moderate these relationships.

Research questions

  • RQ1: Does the richness of sensorimotor simulation in dream reports predict paranormal belief?
  • RQ2: Does simulated supernatural or agent content in dreams predict religiosity?
  • RQ3: How do REM sleep dynamics relate to, or moderate, these associations?

Hypotheses

  • H1: Higher simulation richness in dreams positively predicts RPBS scores.
  • H2: Simulated supernatural or agent content positively predicts religiosity (BMMRS).
  • H3: REM-rich sleep is associated with greater simulation richness and strengthens the above relationships.

Design & measures

  • Secondary analysis of a 10–14-day longitudinal study (n=120 adults; ~1,200 dreams).
  • DREEM subsample (n=60) contributes REM percentage and sleep metrics.
  • Dream coding for sensorimotor detail, agent presence, and narrative structure.
  • Outcomes include religiosity (BMMRS) and paranormal belief (RPBS); regression models with relevant covariates.

Contribution

  • Provides a mechanistic, embodied account of agent concept formation in dreams.
  • Bridges decentering processes in REM sleep with grounded cognition theory.
  • Clarifies the limits of hypersensitive agency detection models by specifying simulation processes tied to valuation and behavior.
